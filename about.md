I'm Paul Perez, an aerospace engineer specialized in planetary sciences, currently looking for the next challenge to take on! I've recently worked as a scientific researcher in atmospheric modelling in the Fluid Dynamics Department at the Royal Military Academy of Belgium, under Prof. Bart Janssens. My project revolved around the development of a web-based tool for near real-time particle dispersion modelling and forecasting of chemical, biological, radiological or nuclear (CBRN) releases, for enhanced emergency response. The main challenges were to reduce the retrieval time of high resolution meteorological datasets needed for the dispersion models, quantify uncertainties for more informed decision making, as well as to create an intuitive interface to reduce the level of expertise usually needed to operate these types of software. Having studied planetary processes through the lens of numerical modelling (see more details about projects I've worked on in the [Projects](/projects/) page), I now look forward to adventuring more upstream, to tackle the tangible engineering challenges of remote sensing and satellite observation missions.

## Background

I hold an MSc in Aerospace Engineering with a Space Flight specialization from the TU Delft (2023), where my interest for planetary and Earth sciences really took shape, bringing together topics that deeply fascinated and resonated with me: the relation between the incredibly small physical phenomena and the large scale dynamics of systems, the environmental and societal challenges of climate change, and the engineering puzzles of satellite missions. I learned how to explore these fields through scientific programming, with projects like my internship at the Netherlands Institute for Space Research (SRON) where I studied uncertainties in modelled atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations. My thesis project then ingrained in me the rigorous and problem-solving approach of applied and experimental research, investigating whether the phenomenon of "booming" sand dunes could exist on Mars, and how it could impact extra-terrestrial exploration and soil characterization on the red planet. These first experiences only strengthened my curiosity about planetary sciences, and have motivated me to pursue a career in the field. It has led me to use programming languages like Julia and Python, study and implement atmospheric numerical models (WRF, FLEXPART), handle and extract meteorological data, and organise my code with Git and GitHub. Maybe most importantly, I have learned to tackle and structure multidisciplinary projects and to process data into results and insights.

I like to take courses for skills or topics I am less familiar with. I've recently completed EUMETSAT's course on the use of Artificial Intelligence for Earth Monitoring, and am eager to delve deeper into the applications of this new technology in my fields. I am also currently following NASA's online course on Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) and its applications, as well as some geographic data analysis Python courses, to be better equipped in the remote sensing world.
